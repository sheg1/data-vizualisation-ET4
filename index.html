Data Visualization Final Project

Life Expectancy and Healthcare Spending

Alexandre Fakhfakh, Alexis Van, Issam Atoui (Groupe 12)

What is it about?

This project examines the relationship between life expectancy, a key indicator of population health, and healthcare spending, which reflects national investment in medical systems.

This topic is vital because healthcare access is unequal; differences in resources and public funding create large global health inequalities. Studying this helps highlight how access varies between countries.

How does healthcare spending per person affect life expectancy in different countries?

Visualization 01: Global Spending vs Life Expectancy

This interactive bubble chart maps the relationship between healthcare spending per capita (X axis, log scale) and life expectancy (Y axis) across all countries. Each bubble represents a country, colored by world region and sized by population. A year slider allows exploration of how this relationship evolves over time.

Extended Analysis: The Global Pattern

The visualization reveals a clear positive logarithmic relationship between healthcare spending and life expectancy. Countries that invest more in healthcare per person tend to have significantly higher life expectancy — but this relationship is not linear.

At the lower end of spending (under $200 per capita), countries show wide variation in life expectancy, ranging from 45 to 70 years. This suggests that at low spending levels, other factors such as political stability, sanitation infrastructure, and disease prevalence play a dominant role.

At the higher end of spending (above $3,000 per capita), countries cluster tightly between 75 and 85 years of life expectancy. This convergence demonstrates that beyond a certain threshold, additional spending yields diminishing improvements.

The regional color encoding highlights stark geographic disparities: European countries dominate the top-right quadrant (high spending, high life expectancy), while African nations cluster in the bottom-left (low spending, low life expectancy). North American countries sit at the far right with the highest spending levels. Asian countries show the widest spread, reflecting the enormous economic diversity within the continent.

The population-sized bubbles add an important dimension: large countries like China and India represent billions of people whose health outcomes are directly tied to national spending decisions, making their position on this chart particularly impactful from a global health perspective.

Visual Encodings: X position = health spending per capita (log scale, PPP $), Y position = life expectancy (years), bubble size = population, color = world region. Interactive filter for year selection via slider.

Do countries that spend more on healthcare see diminishing returns regarding increases in life expectancy?

Visualization 01: The Healthcare Plateau

Extended Analysis: The Saturation Point

This visualization confirms a logarithmic-style relationship between spending and longevity. At early stages of development, small investments in healthcare lead to dramatic jumps in life expectancy, likely due to fundamental improvements in sanitation and basic medical access. However, as shown by the plateauing area, once spending reaches a certain threshold, the curve flattens. This visual evidence supports the theory that money alone cannot indefinitely extend human life; instead, it reaches a "saturation point" where additional dollars yield smaller and smaller benefits.

Visualization 02: Marginal Gains Efficiency

Extended Analysis: Quantifying Diminishing Returns

By calculating the marginal gain, the years of life "bought" by each additional $1,000, we can pinpoint where efficiency drops. The transition from high-gain tiers to diminishing return tiers is striking. While the first $1,000 adds nearly two decades of life, the return for the wealthiest nations drops significantly. In some high-spending cases, the gain is negligible or even slightly negative, suggesting that beyond a certain budget, systemic inefficiencies or lifestyle-related health issues become more influential than medical expenditure itself.

Visual Encodings: We used Area and Line marks for the plateau to emphasize the global trend, and Bar marks with a diverging color scale to highlight the transition from efficient to inefficient spending zones.

In what ways do income levels and healthcare access affect the link between health spending and life expectancy?

Visualization 01: Income, Healthcare Access & Life Expectancy

This interactive bubble chart encodes four dimensions simultaneously: health spending (X axis, log scale), life expectancy (Y axis), GDP per capita (color — representing income level), and UHC Service Coverage Index (bubble size — representing healthcare access). Users can filter by year and region to explore patterns within specific contexts.

Key Terms
  • GDP (Gross Domestic Product) per capita: The total economic output of a country divided by its population — a measure of income level.
  • UHC (Universal Health Coverage) Index: A score from 0 to 100 measuring how well a population can access essential health services (vaccines, doctors, hospitals, treatments).
  • PPP (Purchasing Power Parity): An adjustment that makes spending comparable across countries by accounting for differences in cost of living.
Extended Analysis: The Double Advantage

This visualization reveals a "double advantage" pattern: countries with both high income (warm colors) and strong healthcare access (large bubbles) consistently achieve the highest life expectancy. Conversely, countries with low GDP and low UHC cluster in the bottom-left corner with the lowest life expectancy.

However, the most insightful finding lies in the outliers. Some countries with moderate spending but high UHC scores (large bubbles in the middle of the X axis) achieve life expectancy levels comparable to much wealthier nations. This suggests that how healthcare resources are distributed (UHC) matters as much as — or more than — how much is spent overall.

Filtering by region reveals distinct patterns: in Africa, even small increases in UHC correspond to significant life expectancy gains. In Europe, where UHC is universally high, the variation in life expectancy is more closely tied to GDP differences.

Visualization 02: Country Profile Comparison (Radar Chart)

This radar chart allows a direct comparison of two countries across four key dimensions: Health Spending, Life Expectancy, GDP per capita, and UHC Index. The logarithmic normalization ensures that all variables are visually comparable regardless of their original scale.

Extended Analysis: Why Profiles Matter

The radar chart shifts the analysis from correlation to explanation. By comparing two countries side by side, we can see why one country outperforms another in life expectancy.

For example, comparing France vs Algeria shows that France dominates on all four axes — spending, life expectancy, GDP, and UHC — creating a much larger polygon. But the most revealing comparisons involve countries that break the expected pattern:

  • A country with high GDP but low UHC may have lower life expectancy than expected — showing that wealth alone is not enough.
  • A country with low GDP but high UHC may outperform expectations — showing that efficient healthcare access can compensate for lower income.

This directly answers our research question: income levels and healthcare access independently and jointly affect the spending–life expectancy relationship, and the radar chart makes these interactions visible at the individual country level.

Visual Encodings (Bubble Chart): X position = health spending (log scale), Y position = life expectancy, bubble size = UHC index (healthcare access, power scale), color = GDP per capita (income level, log scale with "turbo" scheme). Interactive filters for year and region.

Visual Encodings (Radar Chart): Radial axes = four normalized dimensions (spending, life exp, GDP, UHC). Polygon area = overall country profile. Color = country identity (blue vs red). Log normalization for spending and GDP to ensure visual comparability.

Data Methodology

Our research utilizes comprehensive datasets:

  • Current health expenditure per capita (PPP $): Adjusted for purchasing power parity to enable fair cross-country comparison.
  • Life expectancy at birth: Measured in total years.
  • GDP per capita (PPP $): Gross Domestic Product per person, adjusted for purchasing power.
  • UHC Service Coverage Index: WHO indicator (0–100) measuring access to essential health services.

Sources: Our World in Data (OWID), World Bank — World Development Indicators, World Health Organization (WHO).
