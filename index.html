Life Expectancy and Healthcare Spending

Alexandre Fakhfakh, Alexis Van, Issam Atoui (Groupe 12)

What is it about?

This project examines the relationship between life expectancy, a key indicator of population health, and healthcare spending, which reflects national investment in medical systems.

This topic is vital because healthcare access is unequal; differences in resources and public funding create large global health inequalities. Studying this helps highlight how access varies between countries.

Do countries that spend more on healthcare see diminishing returns regarding increases in life expectancy?

Visualization 01: The Healthcare Plateau

Extended Analysis: The Saturation Point

This visualization confirms a logarithmic-style relationship between spending and longevity. At early stages of development, small investments in healthcare lead to dramatic jumps in life expectancy, likely due to fundamental improvements in sanitation and basic medical access. However, as shown by the plateauing area, once spending reaches a certain threshold, the curve flattens. This visual evidence supports the theory that money alone cannot indefinitely extend human life; instead, it reaches a "saturation point" where additional dollars yield smaller and smaller benefits.

Visualization 02: Marginal Gains Efficiency

Extended Analysis: Quantifying Diminishing Returns

By calculating the marginal gain, the years of life "bought" by each additional $1,000, we can pinpoint where efficiency drops. The transition from high-gain tiers to diminishing return tiers is striking. While the first $1,000 adds nearly two decades of life, the return for the wealthiest nations drops significantly. In some high-spending cases, the gain is negligible or even slightly negative, suggesting that beyond a certain budget, systemic inefficiencies or lifestyle-related health issues become more influential than medical expenditure itself.

Visual Encodings: We used Area and Line marks for the plateau to emphasize the global trend, and Bar marks with a diverging color scale to highlight the transition from efficient to inefficient spending zones.

Our research utilizes comprehensive datasets:

These datasets are sufficient to explore relationship trends, compare income groups, and analyze several decades of global health evolution.
